## AI Mirrors - An Analysis of Self; Comparing Claude, Gemini, and GPT

I did something interesting today: I asked 3 AI models to read through all my blog posts and tell me what kind of person I am. Before diving into these results, let me provide some background. Models compared were Claude 4.5, Gemini 3, and GPT 5.1.

## Leading Up to This
Recently I've been practicing using AI effectively as a leader in my career and in my life as a sense of duty for the unique position I find myself in. I started a side project in October as a framework for this sort of practice so that I can bring my findings to my team and improve our workflows and processes as new tools become available. The project is self-documenting, as I have identified seemingly endless opportunities to be more productive using AI as a thought partner and assistant to accomplishing almost any personal goal. With these tools, I have found my hour or two of coffee shop time can produce profound output, to the point where I can't justify taking zero action because of time constraints. In one of these learning sessions, I began using the tools to see how capable they were at revamping this site, and curiosity hit me. This site has multiple posts over the years—what insights might it provide on my personality and myself personally? After asking it to read the posts and give me an assessment, I was taken aback by what it had to say. I stepped away for about a week, and came back to this for an experiment, fitting with the theme of what this site was meant for from the beginning. The synergies between my goals in all aspects of career and life, and investigating these fascinating tools, are profound.

## The Results: Comparing Claude, Gemini, and GPT
I used Claude Sonnet 4.5 to compare the three assessments and identify key similarities and differences. After reading the assessments, I felt it was probably the best for the sort of information I was after. Its more human and friendly response had the best tone for what I am after with this post. The comparison seemed to reflect how I felt as I read through all three and was trying to decide which I preferred, but ultimately I see value in using all three as if they are a team or group of friends who have my interests in mind, but from slightly different angles.

**Authenticity**: You write honestly about struggles - depression, loneliness, self-doubt. There's no pretense; you share both insights and uncertainties.

**Self-Disciplined**: Your commitment to daily meditation, regular yoga practice, and consistent blogging shows dedication to chosen practices.

**Quietly Ambitious**: You set goals (learning new skills, career transitions, physical challenges) but seem more focused on personal mastery than external validation.

**Somewhat Introverted**: References to solitude, introspection, and the richness of inner life suggest you recharge through alone time, though you also value meaningful connection.

**Pragmatic Idealist**: You're drawn to philosophical concepts but always ground them in practical application - how to actually live these ideas day-to-day.

### Life Journey

**Physical Practice**: Deep commitment to Ashtanga yoga - not just as exercise but as a philosophical practice and metaphor for life.

**Creative Expression**: Theater and acting represent important outlets for exploring different aspects of self and connecting with others.

- **Concise yet profound** - short posts that pack philosophical depth
- **Question-driven** - you often pose questions to yourself and readers
- **Experiential** - grounded in personal experience rather than abstract theory
- **Honest** - vulnerable about struggles and uncertainties

### In Summary

You're a **thoughtful, intellectually curious person seeking balance between thinking and doing, between solitude and connection, between ambition and contentment**. You value simplicity, presence, and authentic experience over complexity and performance. You're someone who uses practices (meditation, yoga, writing) as tools for understanding yourself and navigating life with more awareness and less suffering.

## Gemini 3 Pro's Assessment

### The Spiritual Technologist
You embody a rare archetype: the **Engineer-Monk**. You approach the ineffable questions of existence—"Who am I?", "What is my purpose?"—with the rigorous toolkit of a developer. You don't just practice yoga; you study its "Eight Limbs" as a system architecture for living. You resonate with stories like *The Surrender Experiment* because they validate your dual nature: a mind built for logic finding its home in the surrender of meditation.

### Debugging the Self
Your writing reveals a person who treats their own psychology as a codebase in need of constant refactoring. In posts like "Who am I" and "Numb," you document your "bugs"—the critical inner voice, the feelings of unworthiness—and then you deploy "patches" in the form of mindfulness, witnessing, and routine. You are not afraid to look at the error logs of your life; in fact, you publish them.

### Structured Vulnerability
There is a distinct contrast between your emotional depth and your delivery. You often use bullet points, lists, and clear headings to discuss messy, painful topics like depression, loneliness, and pandemic anxiety. This suggests you find safety in structure. You are a **Pragmatic Idealist** who builds rigid containers (routines, lists, blog formats) to hold your fluid and sometimes overwhelming emotions.

### The Forward-Looking Humanist
While you are deeply introspective, you are not a Luddite. You embrace AI and new technology ("Embracing the Power of AI") not as replacements for humanity, but as tools to augment it. However, you maintain a "watchdog" perspective, worrying about how these tools affect society. You are a **Conscious Early Adopter**, willing to try the new beta version of the future while keeping a close eye on the terms of service.

## GPT-5.1 Assessment

### Core Identity

**A Reflective Builder**: Across your posts, you come through as someone who is continually trying to understand yourself and then quietly rebuild your life around what you learn. You’re not just asking “Who am I?” as a philosophical exercise—you’re using that question to guide how you work, train, rest, and relate to others.

**Seeker in Practice, Not Just Theory**: You gravitate toward big themes—identity, meaning, presence, “enoughness”—but you always pull them back down into concrete actions: going to class, cleaning your office, adjusting a routine, writing a short post instead of waiting for the perfect one. Your search is lived, not just thought.

### Values & Philosophy

**Presence and Honesty**: You care a lot about being present with your own experience, even when it’s uncomfortable. You write openly about depression, numbness, anxiety, and self-criticism without dramatizing them. There’s a consistent preference for “true and imperfect” over “polished but false.”

**Growth Through Disciplines**: Yoga, running, meditation, writing, and even coding function as disciplines rather than hobbies. You return to them as ways to notice patterns, soften unhelpful habits, and keep yourself moving—physically, mentally, emotionally.

**Simplicity and Essentialism**: You’re drawn to stripping things down to what matters: fewer distractions, fewer possessions, fewer pretenses. You look for simple practices that can be repeated over time rather than complicated once-and-done solutions.

### Personality Traits

**Introspective and Analytical**: You naturally turn inward and examine your own thoughts, beliefs, and reactions. But you also analyze them—naming patterns, listing them out (“Here’s how I see myself”), and comparing different frameworks (personality types, yoga philosophy, etc.).

**Emotionally Sensitive, Quietly Resilient**: You feel things deeply—loneliness, confusion, grief, doubt—but your response is rarely to collapse. Instead, you acknowledge what’s there, give it language, and then look for the next small, workable step. There’s a calm kind of toughness in that.

**Structure-Seeking**: When life feels chaotic, you tend to reach for structure: lists, routines, series of posts, training plans, frameworks like the eight limbs of yoga. These don’t erase your emotions, but they give you a scaffolding to work with them more safely.

**Introverted Connector**: You seem to recharge in solitude and reflection, yet relationships clearly matter a great deal: family shifts, friendships, teachers, partners, communities like yoga studios. You don’t need a crowd, but you care deeply about a few meaningful connections.

### Life Journey Themes

**Mind–Body Integration**: Your posts show an ongoing effort to bring your mind (analysis, reflection, stories) and body (running, yoga, cold ocean dips, fatigue, injury) into some kind of alignment. You’re interested in what your body is telling you, not just what you think about it.

**Career as One Dimension, Not the Whole Story**: You take your work seriously and are proud of how your skills have grown, but you don’t reduce yourself to your job title. Career shows up as one thread in a larger weave of practice, creativity, service, and inner work.

**Technology as Tool, Not Identity**: You’re curious about AI and software and clearly capable, but you seem more interested in how these tools can support understanding, creativity, or clarity than in chasing tech for its own sake. There’s a healthy distance—engaged but watchful.

### Writing Style

Your writing tends to be:
- **Concise and grounded**: You favor short, direct posts that point to lived experience rather than long theoretical essays.
- **List- and pattern-oriented**: You often reach for lists, bullet points, or structured sections to organize complex or painful topics.
- **Candid and unpretentious**: You don’t try to present a “brand.” You sound like yourself, whether you’re inspired, stuck, or somewhere in between.
- **Process-focused**: Many entries feel like snapshots in an ongoing process rather than polished conclusions. You let readers see you mid-journey.

### In Summary

You come across as a **thoughtful, emotionally aware builder**—someone who uses practices, structures, and honest reflection to navigate the ongoing work of being human. You live at the intersection of introspection and pragmatism: willing to sit with hard questions, but also willing to lace up your shoes, roll out the mat, open the editor, or write the next small post.

You seem like the kind of person who may never feel like you’ve “arrived,” but who keeps showing up anyway—with curiosity, humility, and a quiet commitment to becoming a little more present, a little more whole, over time.
